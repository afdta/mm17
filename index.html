The Metro Monitor provides a comprehensive framework for measuring successful economic outcomes in metropolitan America.

The Monitor benchmarks regional economies on economic growth, prosperity, and inclusion and it provides the foundation for a forthcoming series of research aimed at helping leaders understand the factors that affect these facets of the changing economy. The understanding this provides will be useful in the development of new models of economic development that can help deliver an advanced economy that works for all.

Growth indicators capture change in the total size of a metropolitan area’s economy. As a metropolitan economy grows, it creates new opportunities for individuals and can become more efficient as it grows larger. Growth indicators capture change in:

Jobs

Total full- and part-time jobs. Source: Moody's Analytics

Output

Total value of goods and services produced in a metropolitan economy. Source: Moody's Analytics

Firm births

New firm starts...

Prosperity refers to the wealth and income produced by an economy on a per-capita or per-worker basis. When a metropolitan area grows by increasing the productivity of its workers, the value of those workers’ labor rises. As the value of labor rises, so can workers’ wages. In this way, prosperity indicators capture the quality of a metropolitan area’s economic growth from the standpoint of its workers and residents. Prosperity indicators capture change in:

Output per person

A measure of living standards equal to GMP divided by total population. Source: Moody's Analytics and the U.S. Census Bureau

Output per job

A measure of productivity equal to GMP divided by total jobs. Source: Moody's Analytics

Average wage

Aggregate wages divided by total jobs. Source: Moody's Analytics

Inclusion indicators measure how the benefits of growth and prosperity in a metropolitan economy are distributed among people. Inclusive growth enables more people to invest in their skills and to purchase more goods and services. Inclusion indicators capture change in:

Employment to population ratio

The share of all individuals aged 18 to 65 who are employed. Source: U.S. Census Bureau

Median wage

The annual wage earned by the person in the very middle of a metropolitan area's income distribution. Source: U.S. Census Bureau

Relative poverty

The share of people in a metropolitan economy who earn less than half of the local median wage. Source: U.S. Census Bureau

Growth, prosperity, and inclusion often do not go hand-in-hand

The chart below depicts the combined z-scores of all metros on growth, prosperity, and inclusion (in that order). The dark lines connect the metro areas that perform above average on all three. The chart will default to this view of the data, but the user can also interact with it to see how other metro areas throughout the three distributions fared. We can also set other views that the user could toggle through (e.g. top 20 on growth).

The other alternative I've considered here is a series of maps that the user would scroll through, starting with above average growth, then above average growth and prosperity, and finally above average on all three. This would allow the user to see how places "drop off" the map as you consider each category. The map would stay fixed on the screen and text annotating each map would scroll over it (example).
